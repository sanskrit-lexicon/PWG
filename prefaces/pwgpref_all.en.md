## Page 02 — Foreword, 1

<sub>Source (scan): [pwg1-0000--02.png](https://sanskrit-lexicon.uni-koeln.de/scans/csldev/csldoc/build/dictionaries/prefaces/pwgpref/pwgpref02.html)</sub>

[page III]

FOREWORD.

In the thirty-odd years that have passed since H. H. Wilson's Sanskrit–English Dictionary appeared in Calcutta, the study of the Sanskrit language and literature has made such powerful advances among us that the attempt to give firmer supports and pillars, through a new treatment of the vocabulary, to an edifice that is ever spreading further and growing higher may well be timely.

The important work which the famous English scholar then (1819) brought about, supported by the labours of Indian pandits, and which he published a second time thirteen years later (1832) in enlarged and improved form, has met the demands that could be made in that age and the purpose it strove to attain. No one has been able to dispense with it, and it is impossible to see how, without this book, the knowledge of Sanskrit literature could have reached the point at which it stands today. None the less, it has remained far from the goal that is set for every dictionary: strictly speaking, it is only a collection of words and word-meanings, but no real treatment of the vocabulary. We admire the extraordinary familiarity of the Nestor of Indianists with the so-called classical language and literature, but we regret that, in the dictionary as in the grammar, he preferred to remain at the standpoint of the Indian scholars rather than to set out upon the path demanded by European scholarship. Our book too, despite all efforts, cannot wholly rise above the preparatory stage, but we nevertheless advance one step closer to the goal.

Our dictionary consists of two elements not entirely resolvable into one another: the material of the word-collections made by learned Indians, and our own collections drawn from Sanskrit literature. Had it been possible to exploit and work through all the works of Sanskrit literature that have come down to our time, then the material of the Indian word-collections — which is in any case poor and slight in proportion to the entire vocabulary of the language — might have been set aside without any considerable harm, and it would not have been necessary to repeat the countless errors and distortions which Indian scholarship presents to us. But since for this both the means and the resources were equally lacking, it lay within our task to supplement the deficiency of our own collections with the store of those, and in return to accept their defects into the bargain as well.

In the book itself one will find examples enough of the strange contradictions in which Indian lexicography stands with demonstrable usage; still more numerous are the cases in which it attains the truth only by half. And one can scarcely deny the probability of an error by pointing, say, to some other usage in another source not accessible to us or not taken into account by us, precisely because in the error there still lies an unmistakable pointing toward the truth. But just as, on the word of an ancient Brâhmaṇa, one believes what one sees oneself, and not the words of others — even of many — who assure the contrary, so here too the glance into the books before us must be more powerful than the testimony of the glossators.

We must record these contradictions and divergences, must attend to the residue of Indian lexicography that does not resolve into actual usage, even to the words evidently distorted by the glossator out of unfamiliarity with the texts, in order not to sweep out, here and there along with the rubble, a usable piece which we are not rich enough to do without.

It is from this point of view that we wish our procedure with the words and word-explanations handed down by the grammarians and lexicographers to be regarded.

## Page 03 — Foreword, 2

<sub>Source (scan): [pwg1-0000--03.png](https://sanskrit-lexicon.uni-koeln.de/scans/csldev/csldoc/build/dictionaries/prefaces/pwgpref/pwgpref03.html)</sub>

[page IV]

FOREWORD.

On the other hand, we have striven to give our own collections a greater extent, and thereby to lay the only firm ground for lexicography. A special list at the end of the Foreword enumerates the printed and manuscript works which have been excerpted by us, partly in full, partly with a selection of the characteristic and more important matter, and partly only in passing.

In particular, we have sought to win the right beginning for our work through a complete treatment of the most important books of Vedic literature. With this an entirely new field is opened, from whose yield a hitherto scarcely suspected wealth flows into the Sanskrit vocabulary. And the gain is not limited to this increase alone; it is just as considerable for the understanding of the whole body of the language. From there run out the threads of the concepts, which one is able to follow in their interlacings only if one holds fast to their beginning. Or, where in later usage the fundamental conception of a word has been effaced, broken apart as it were into seemingly unconnected pieces, like a worn or shattered coin, there one may reach back into the treasure of the Veda to find still the full, undamaged stamp and the statement of the original value.

Many roots which later disappear entirely, rich word-families which have died out down to a few descendants, one finds here alive and flourishing, and through them one learns to understand their later offshoots as well. For this reason etymology too is advanced especially by the unlocking of the ancient language, and comparative philology ought to hold itself far more strictly than has hitherto been the case to the now demonstrable oldest stock of the vocabulary.

What the Greek lexicon would lack if it began with Herodotus and the great tragedians and left out of account everything older — that same thing, and much more, the greater the mass of ancient literature is, is lacking in the Sanskrit dictionary which sets aside the books gathered under the wide name of the Veda.

To be sure, what H. T. Colebrooke said fifty years ago has come to pass — that the difficulty of the Vedic language would long stand in the way of a more exact investigation drawing out from this extensive literature all that is worth knowing; but the labours of the last decades have nevertheless made this primeval forest passable to such a degree that the attempt at exploration undertaken by us will not remain without result. One might call this attempt a bold one, because a dictionary is to be created before exegesis has even seriously begun its work: one demands the conclusion and the result of a reckoning before the individual factors of it have been established. For it must be acknowledged that almost all the exegetical preliminary work that exists is more apt to mislead us than to lead us in difficult matters. With the exception of Th. Benfey's work on the Sâmaveda, nothing extensive has yet been brought to light that would satisfy the most general demands of a scientific exegesis, and even this predecessor we may not always follow. Our business would already be as good as half done if Langlois' alleged translation of the Ṛgveda deserved that name in any way. But it deserves it only for those passages in which it would be hard even for a beginner to go wrong, and scarcely even for these, since it takes pains everywhere to force the most perverse sense upon its text.

The spirit of the Veda, with its simple and lofty thoughts, was here forced through the range of ideas of an interpreter in whom it was unable to strike even a single spark — an interpreter who, weak in his knowledge of the language and visibly disdaining the necessary preparations, has lost many years upon a work that went far beyond his powers.

Were Langlois' book merely useless, then the bequest of the now departed might be set aside and covered with the veil of oblivion; but because in unskilled hands it works harmfully and must obscure and impede the understanding of Indian antiquity just as Anquetil's translation of the Zendavesta has impeded insight into the Iranian past, a decided testimony must be given against it: it must be denied each and every authority. And this testimony is needed, because men who might well have been quite familiar with the state of affairs have, for whatever reasons, not spoken this truth in their public judgments.

If only scanty aids for the understanding of Vedic texts are offered us by the moderns, we are all the more directed to that which Indian scholarship itself has done for their explanation — to the commentaries. And indeed, for the one of the two parts of Vedic literature, for the books of theology and of cult, we could wish for no better guides than these commentators, exact in every particular, who follow their texts word for word, who do not tire of repeating everywhere what has often already been said, wherever even a semblance of misunderstanding might arise, and who sometimes seem to have written rather for us strangers than for their own, grown up amid these conceptions and impressions,

## Page 04 — Foreword, 3

<sub>Source (scan): [pwg1-0000--04.png](https://sanskrit-lexicon.uni-koeln.de/scans/csldev/csldoc/build/dictionaries/prefaces/pwgpref/pwgpref04.html)</sub>

[page V]

priestly pupils. Here they are on their own ground, where the task is to explain the ramified, ingenious, often subtle symbolism of the cult, to elucidate all the innumerable minutiae upon whose observance in worship eternal salvation or perdition depends. For in the Brâhmaṇa there breathes the very same spirit that has worked its way down through the whole of orthodox Brahmanical theology and has filled in particular those Brahman schools which, a few centuries ago, strove so eagerly to investigate and explain the outstanding works of their ancient theological literature.

It is, however, quite another matter when these same men set themselves the task of explaining the ancient hymn collections. These texts are not creations of a theological speculation, nor did they grow from the soil of that firmly circumscribed, minute liturgical practice; rather, for the greatest part they are products of the oldest religious lyric, whose artistic practice was as little bound to families or castes as was the offering of the daily sacrifice and prayer. In them there lives a world of gods, and a cult is mirrored, which differ essentially from the system taught in the Brâhmaṇa; they speak a language separated from the language of the Brâhmaṇa — which is scarcely anything other than so-called classical Sanskrit — by as great a gulf as the Latin of the Salian hymns from that of M. Terentius Varro. Here, therefore, not only were quite different skills required for interpretation, but also freedom of judgement, a greater breadth of horizon, and historical perspective. But freedom of judgement has, among all peoples of pagan antiquity, been wanting in priestly learning, and of historical development one has never known anything in India.

The very same qualities, then, by which those commentators became excellent guides to the understanding of the theological books, make them unfit guides in that far older and quite differently constituted domain. Since so-called classical Sanskrit was perfectly familiar to them, they sought its usage also in the Vedic hymn; since the order of the rite appeared to them as not at all conceivable otherwise and as existing from the beginning of the world, those forefathers of Indian worship of the gods must likewise have sacrificed in just this way; since the order of the gods and the world system of their own time were to them inviolable and revealed truths, these same truths must necessarily be found again in the very core of revelation, in the hymns of the ancient Ṛshi, who, after all, had lived in intimate intercourse with the gods and had been partakers of a far higher wisdom than the later-born generations.

It is not necessary to support this state of affairs with examples and to elaborate it further, and just as little will one expect to find here more closely indicated the nevertheless very considerable gain which accrues to us from the works of these interpreters, in spite of all their imperfections. The whole relationship is neither hard to recognize nor unique of its kind. Thus the sacred books of the ancient peoples were explained by later centuries according to the prevailing systems of theology and according to the higher or lower state of learning in general, and everywhere in such a way that this interpretation gave itself out as tradition, that is, claimed for itself an antiquity and a dignity of which it could not in truth always boast. Nor has it occurred to anyone, for example, to make our understanding of the Hebrew books of the Old Testament dependent on the Talmud and the Rabbis, whereas there is no lack of those who hold it to be the duty of a conscientious interpreter of the Veda that he should translate according to Sâjaṇa, Mahîdhara, and others. We therefore do not believe, as H. H. Wilson *) does, that, for instance, Sâjaṇa understood the expressions of the Veda better than any European interpreter, so that we would have nothing further to do than to repeat after him; rather, we believe that a conscientious European interpreter can understand the Veda far more correctly and better than Sâjaṇa; we do not regard it as the immediate task to attain that understanding of the Veda which was current in India some centuries ago **), but rather seek the meaning which the poets themselves placed in their hymns and sayings. We accordingly hold that the writings of Sâjaṇa and the other commentators are not a guiding standard for the interpreter, but only one of the aids which he must employ for the solution of his task — a task that is admittedly difficult and not to be solved at the first onset nor by a single person. In this respect we have greatly regretted that Müller's meritorious edition of the commentary on the Ṛgveda has not already advanced further.

We have therefore attempted to follow the path which linguistic science prescribes: to win their meaning from the texts themselves by holding together all passages related in wording or content; a slow and laborious path, on which indeed neither the commentators nor the translators have gone before us. For this reason the double task both of the exegete and of the lexicographer has fallen to us. The etymological procedure alone, as it must be practised by those

## Page 05 — Foreword, 4

<sub>Source (scan): [pwg1-0000--05.png](https://sanskrit-lexicon.uni-koeln.de/scans/csldev/csldoc/build/dictionaries/prefaces/pwgpref/pwgpref05.html)</sub>

who strive to divine the meaning of a word without having before their eyes, besides the passage that lies before them, the ten or twenty others in which the same word recurs, can never lead to the correct goal. Even when practised according to linguistic laws, it moves in far too wide logical circles to hit the right point each time, and produces far too general, colourless concepts which perhaps contain within themselves the firmly circumscribed, sharply defined content of the word, but do not render it in its particularity and thereby in its force and beauty.

This is the procedure in which the commentators went before us, and with which they have furnished the surest proof that they had not mastered the entire vocabulary of these books at one and the same time, and likewise that they did not work from a fixed traditional explanation for the individual passages. Hence it comes about that they have explained a countless number of words of the Veda with *force, sacrifice, food, wisdom*, and numerous verbs, for example, with *to go, to move*, all of which, differing among themselves, have a definite value and concrete content and often scarcely even remotely brush against those general concepts. And only by reinstating these misunderstood words in their lost right does the Veda gain striking sense, force, and richness of expression, and give us a quite different picture of the thought-world of the earliest antiquity.

Whoever knows the difficulties of such an undertaking will not refuse us indulgence for our doubtless numerous missteps — missteps which, in the course of the work, will reveal themselves first and most clearly to ourselves. And this part of the dictionary, being the newest, will also be the first to become outdated; for the combined labour of many able workers directing themselves to the Veda will very rapidly advance the understanding of it and determine many things more truly and more exactly than we were able to do at the first attempt. Centuries have laboured at the lexical understanding of Homer, and yet his vocabulary is not exhaustively explained, and yet Homer presents, on the linguistic side, incomparably fewer difficulties than the Vedic hymn. How could one have hoped to recast without delay these monuments of an early antiquity — which confronts us nowhere else than here, fixed in writing — into translations, like some piece of modern book-making!

After a good copy of the Taittirîja-Saṁhitâ had been procured for us during the printing through the obliging efforts of Dr. Röer in Calcutta, we have been able to work through all the Vedic Saṁhitâ, and have taken care to exploit fully those of the Ṛk and the Atharvan, the Vâǵasaneji-Saṁhitâ, and the proper portions of the Sâmaveda, and also to record the compounds; a procedure which was not extended to the Taittirîja-Saṁhitâ, since in its main content it belongs not to the hymn collections but to the class of writings of the Brâhmaṇa, for which the same completeness of recording could not be achieved without taking up too much space for an inessential gain.

We receive a valuable support through a complete and exact word-index to the Atharvan, which Professor W. D. Whitney in New Haven, Connecticut, supplies to us.

The inclusion of the Brâhmaṇa and Sûtra literature in the considerable extent which it now occupies in the dictionary has become possible only because Dr. Albrecht Weber in Berlin furnished us with the rich material of the Çatapathabrâhmaṇa, of the Sûtra of Kâtjâjana, and subsequently also that of the Çâṅkhâjana and the Pâraskara. Without this assistance we would, at least for the beginning of the work, have been restricted to the Aitarejabrâhmaṇa, to the two Sûtra of Âçvalâjana, as well as to those of Kauçika belonging to the Atharvaveda. We also regard it as a pleasant duty to declare publicly that Weber, from the first moment until now, has with his characteristic warmth sought in every way to advance this undertaking begun with united forces.

Dr. Adalbert Kuhn has begun to exploit Çâṅkhâjana's Gṛhjasûtra for our dictionary.

Dr. Aufrecht, before his removal to Oxford, supported us in the Vedic domain up to अग्र excl.

To Professor Stenzler we must express our thanks for the relinquishing of a complete index to the Manu, which contains *all* words with the citation of *all* passages in which they appear; the same scholar has also communicated to us extracts from various unedited Indian dictionaries which are cited by the scholiasts of the Kirâtârguṇija and the Çiçupâlavadha, and finally has left to us for free use a copy of the first edition of Bopp's Glossary, in which rare words and word-meanings from various authors have occasionally been recorded.

To the Academician Schiefner we owe manifold communications from the Buddhist literature, in particular from the Vjutpatti, a Sanskrit-Tibetan dictionary.

The philosophical literature is most weakly represented in our work, and we would receive with particular thanks help in this direction from scholars who are at home in this field.

## Page 06 — Foreword, 5

<sub>Source (scan): [pwg1-0000--07.png](https://sanskrit-lexicon.uni-koeln.de/scans/csldev/csldoc/build/dictionaries/prefaces/pwgpref/pwgpref06.html)</sub>

[page VII]

The generally accessible dictionaries, root collections, and glossaries of Wilson, Haughton, Westergaard, Bopp, Lassen, Gildemeister, and Benfey we have of course used, but we have admitted no word and no meaning into our dictionary without prior examination. Râdhâkânta's Çabdakalpadruma, a work which redounds in many respects to the greatest honour of the learned Indian, has enabled us to use a great number of unedited Indian dictionaries and of commentaries on these dictionaries which were not at our disposal even in manuscript. Citations from edited and unedited writings too, but above all the rich synonymy of plant names, have been of great service to us *). For a copy of this valuable work, unobtainable through the book trade, the Imperial Academy of Sciences is indebted to the generosity of the author.

In order to make searching easier for the users of our dictionary, we have the following to remark. We have completely banished from the verbal roots the vowels ऋ, ॠ, and ऌ, and likewise the diphthongs from their final position; ऋ in the final position of nominal stems we have replaced by अर्. Denominatives which have hitherto been listed in the root form we have given in connexion with the denominative suffix.

The accent we have indicated in the simplest manner, one not, however, current among the Indians. In the examples from the Vedic literature, on the other hand, the manner of marking customary in the manuscripts has been retained. With the verbal roots no class is indicated, since this follows of itself from the appended 3rd person sg. praes., especially when the accent is taken into account.

Our work has been divided in such a way that the one (Roth) works on the literature of the Veda and of the Vedic auxiliary books, as well as the Âjurveda of Suçruta, and besides this directs his attention to the botanical names, while the remaining literature, together with the arrangement of the whole material, is attended to by the other.

Many a deficiency of the dictionary may find its excuse in the fact that its two compilers live at a distance from one another which is too great to permit even occasional meetings. Our work has thereby been not a little hindered. If we nevertheless succeed in keeping up our courage against the inner and outer difficulties of our task — for which the diligence of a decade will not suffice — may one recognize therein a testimony of the zeal with which we are ready to serve science in our field. The same zeal will, we hope, should it not be granted to ourselves to bring to an end what has been begun, awaken successors who will solve the task as well as or better than we.

## Page 15 — Foreword, vol. 2

<sub>Source (scan): [pwg2-0000--04.png](https://sanskrit-lexicon.uni-koeln.de/scans/csldev/csldoc/build/dictionaries/prefaces/pwgpref/pwgpref15.html)</sub>

The second volume of the dictionary, which we herewith present to the public, has been brought to completion in a shorter span of time than the first, even though it contains almost as many printed sheets as the latter. The material has meanwhile increased considerably, as every attentive user of the dictionary will at once perceive. Not only the editors of the dictionary themselves, but also their friend Weber, have year by year enlarged the circle of literary works drawn upon at the beginning of the work; and from the letter ज onward we have moreover received from Dr. Kern in Groenlo, an able pupil of Weber's, very valuable contributions from the works of Varâhamihira, which until now exist only in manuscript.

We originally intended to complete the gutturals, palatals, and so-called cerebrals in the second volume; but the concern that the second volume would thereby become considerably larger than the first, and that its use would be made still more difficult for some time, has determined us to close with the letter ट. The third volume will, so far as one can foresee, bring the dentals to completion and thus conclude the half of the whole work.

St. Petersburg /
Tübingen, 14/26 October 1858.

For the sake of ease of finding, the arrangement would have to be strictly alphabetical (according to the opening words of the saying); for each saying all the passages in which it is communicated would have to be indicated; in addition, it would be the duty of the editor to communicate also the variants, at least the more important ones. FOREWORD.

The compiler of a large dictionary faces a twofold danger: if he reaches too far afield and loses himself too much in particulars, or if he sets about the work only at an advanced age, it may easily happen that his work is left unfinished — and an unfinished dictionary is less useful than any other work that has not been brought to completion. The other danger is that, intent above all on actually bringing his work to an end, he hastens it too much at the expense of accuracy and completeness. If we take the 2nd edition of Wilson's dictionary as our measure, then ⅗ of our work has been accomplished; on this portion we have spent about 12½ years. The author of a comprehensive dictionary lays out his entire store of knowledge and keeps nothing back for himself; if the author of a concise dictionary knows no more than he was able to draw from the scanty material that he places before the reader's eyes, then his work is in a sorry state.

The author of a large dictionary is excused if, on his far-flung travels, he here and there overlooks even something close at hand; from the author of a concise dictionary one rightly demands that he master his small field completely and that, as appointed guide, he not desert the inexperienced traveller — the user of his work — precisely in the moment of need.

In the interest of the users of our dictionary, however, we should wish that the author of a concise dictionary marked everything new that he offers, and also everything old that he construes differently, by appending in such a case, by way of exception, the citations as well — even were this to be done at the expense of those for whom his work is primarily intended. Gildemeister, in the glossary to Lassen's Anthology, has marked with an asterisk words and meanings that are wanting in ours, for which we must thank him.

In a large dictionary a risky etymology or comparison does comparatively little harm, since it is as a rule used only by scholars; a concise dictionary is in the hands of beginners, to whom only what is entirely certain may be offered, so that they may in good time accustom themselves to circumspection and acquire the feeling for what is correct or probable — that feeling, so indispensable to the philologist and independent of all learning.

To append a transcription to the Sanskrit word is an evil, since it makes the book more expensive and brings no benefit. Whoever reads Sanskrit texts must know the Sanskrit alphabet; and whoever does not know the Sanskrit alphabet should not use the dictionary for comparative linguistics. In the field of Indo-Germanic comparative linguistics there are nowadays so many masters that we truly have no need of bunglers. Nor can a concise dictionary in general take the place of a large dictionary for comparative linguists, since it leaves out of account the grist and grain of a word.

The following words are addressed to lexicographers, grammarians, and systematic opponents. To retain, after the manner of the Indian grammarians, ऋ, ॠ, ऌ, ए, ऐ and ओ in roots, while one nevertheless readily abandons other peculiarities of theirs — such as, for example, the use of ण and ष at the beginning of roots that in reality begin with न and स — is in part an offence against the Indian grammarians, whom one makes appear more limited than they in reality were, but is altogether an offence against European scholarship, since one is, to be sure, able to cite Indian grammarians in justification of such spellings, while forgetting that they strove to do justice not to theory but to practice.

To impute to that Indian grammarian who first set up roots in ॠ and ओ that he believed in their actual existence is tantamount to assuming that he did not rightly understand how to distinguish a finished word from a stem (theme), since he sometimes calls the latter too (but for purely practical purposes) पद्. With the mere understanding of a rule of Pâṇini's one has not yet grasped Pâṇini's standpoint in scholarship. Nor does the study of the later grammarians lead us to this insight, but rather an unbiased philological intelligence does. If, for example, Pâṇini, in order to designate the case in which the irregular form आश्चर्य is formed from चर् with आ, says: आश्चर्यमनित्ये, i.e. one forms आश्चर्य when the word has the meaning «unusual», then only a pedant like Kâtjâjana can hit upon the idea of saying that his predecessor ought to have put अद्भुते *marvel* instead of अनित्ये. But if a European scholar attempts to reconcile the apparent contradiction between the two grammarians by assuming that the word आश्चर्य could, in the interval lying between Pâṇini and Kâtjâjana, have undergone a modification of meaning, then it appears as though this scholar were more competent to operate with mathematical quantities than with quantities whose exact value is to be determined only with philological tact. When now, to the grammarian's ingrained whims, there is added passion as well, to darken his insight, then abnormities arise, to oppose which cannot be expected of such people as are concerned with the matter itself.
