# FOREWORD.

After a labour extending over nearly twenty-five years we have brought the dictionary to its conclusion. It has been, even unfinished, in everyone's hands, and people have had time to acquaint themselves with its merits and its shortcomings: we are therefore exempted from the duty of accompanying it with explanations.

Only one thing do we believe we ought to call to mind on this occasion: how very different from today were the state and extent of Sanskrit literature back then, when we began; how much was lacking to us with which one is familiar today; how few preliminary studies were at hand for us or came to our aid in the course of the work.

We nevertheless ventured to direct our aim at the whole of the literature and to make it serviceable to us, so far as our hand just reached. What at the outset was to be had only in fragments has gradually become accessible, often indeed through our prompting, and so the dictionary could, in its progress, grow more and more into a thesaurus. Had we confined ourselves to that literature which lay before us at the beginnings, and wished to be complete in this, then completeness would soon have turned into the most pitiful poverty. We have therefore spread out on all sides, gathered together from everywhere, even if in fragments, whatever was attainable and whatever time and strength sufficed for; we have not shrunk from being uneven, if the matter was thereby served. What has once been registered by us now profits the present user, and in the future the successor, however full of gaps our collection in the field concerned may be.\*)

For the Veda we have reckoned in vain on indices and special dictionaries; everything comes after us. We have been left with the unwished-for honour of precedence right up to the end.

The same faithful collaborators who stood at our side from the beginning have remained, in particular our friend A. Weber, whose collection — purchased with the most disinterested expenditure of precious time and trouble — from the lexically significant category of writings of the Brâhmana and Sûtra, in part accessible and familiar to him alone, redounds to the special ornament of our book. Next to him, H. Kern, A. Fr. Stenzler and W. D. Whitney contributed the most. If we mention A. Schiefner last, we mean thereby only to have said that the Buddhist literature, in which he is especially at home, by its very nature offers material not always to be turned to account for a Sanskrit dictionary.

As we mention this active assistance with thanks, so must we also testify that, on the whole, the encouraging participation, a forbearing and appreciative judgement of all our colleagues in the field, on whose verdict we set store, has accompanied us up to this point.

The patroness of our work, however, the Imperial Academy of Sciences, will not, we hope, withhold from us her approval of the manner in which we have carried out her commission — a commission which a rare favour of fortune permitted us to fulfil completely.

<div style="text-align:center">

Jena and Tübingen, 4 August 1875.

O. Böhtlingk.<br/>
R. Roth.

</div>

---

\*) M. Müller asks in the preface to the 6th volume of his edition of the Ṛgveda, p. X: „Why are not such technical terms (sic) as *gîtin, çirahkampin,* etc., given in the Petersburg Dictionary?“ In our dictionary much is missing, but these two words in particular are not missing. They are to be found in their proper place (not, say, in the addenda), with the correct meaning, and each with the only attestation known up to now. We could therefore rather ask, for our part: how can one censure so frivolously?
