# Foreword

[page VII]

The generally accessible dictionaries, root collections, and glossaries of Wilson, Haughton, Westergaard, Bopp, Lassen, Gildemeister, and Benfey we have of course used, but we have admitted no word and no meaning into our dictionary without prior examination. Râdhâkânta's Çabdakalpadruma, a work which redounds in many respects to the greatest honour of the learned Indian, has enabled us to use a great number of unedited Indian dictionaries and of commentaries on these dictionaries which were not at our disposal even in manuscript. Citations from edited and unedited writings too, but above all the rich synonymy of plant names, have been of great service to us *). For a copy of this valuable work, unobtainable through the book trade, the Imperial Academy of Sciences is indebted to the generosity of the author.

In order to make searching easier for the users of our dictionary, we have the following to remark. We have completely banished from the verbal roots the vowels ऋ, ॠ, and ऌ, and likewise the diphthongs from their final position; ऋ in the final position of nominal stems we have replaced by अर्. Denominatives which have hitherto been listed in the root form we have given in connexion with the denominative suffix.

The accent we have indicated in the simplest manner, one not, however, current among the Indians. In the examples from the Vedic literature, on the other hand, the manner of marking customary in the manuscripts has been retained. With the verbal roots no class is indicated, since this follows of itself from the appended 3rd person sg. praes., especially when the accent is taken into account.

Our work has been divided in such a way that the one (Roth) works on the literature of the Veda and of the Vedic auxiliary books, as well as the Âjurveda of Suçruta, and besides this directs his attention to the botanical names, while the remaining literature, together with the arrangement of the whole material, is attended to by the other.

Many a deficiency of the dictionary may find its excuse in the fact that its two compilers live at a distance from one another which is too great to permit even occasional meetings. Our work has thereby been not a little hindered. If we nevertheless succeed in keeping up our courage against the inner and outer difficulties of our task — for which the diligence of a decade will not suffice — may one recognize therein a testimony of the zeal with which we are ready to serve science in our field. The same zeal will, we hope, should it not be granted to ourselves to bring to an end what has been begun, awaken successors who will solve the task as well as or better than we.

---

*) It has pleased us to hear that the learned Indian intends to make use of our dictionary in a second edition of his work. He writes to us about this from Calcutta, on 17 May 1855, as follows: «As a friend of mine intends availing himself of the aid of your Work, specially as regards the Vaidic Vocabulary, in the preparation, under my superintendence, of a second and more comprehensive Edition of the Sabdakalpadruma — I shall feel myself greatly obliged by your kindly sending me the subsequent parts of the Work and making arrangements to furnish me with the sheets as they come out from the Press per every Mail.» We need hardly remark that, with the consent of the Imperial Academy of Sciences, we have gladly acceded to this wish.
