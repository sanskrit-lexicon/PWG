# Foreword, 1

[page III]

FOREWORD.

In the thirty-odd years that have passed since H. H. Wilson's Sanskrit–English Dictionary appeared in Calcutta, the study of the Sanskrit language and literature has made such powerful advances among us that the attempt to give firmer supports and pillars, through a new treatment of the vocabulary, to an edifice that is ever spreading further and growing higher may well be timely.

The important work which the famous English scholar then (1819) brought about, supported by the labours of Indian pandits, and which he published a second time thirteen years later (1832) in enlarged and improved form, has met the demands that could be made in that age and the purpose it strove to attain. No one has been able to dispense with it, and it is impossible to see how, without this book, the knowledge of Sanskrit literature could have reached the point at which it stands today. None the less, it has remained far from the goal that is set for every dictionary: strictly speaking, it is only a collection of words and word-meanings, but no real treatment of the vocabulary. We admire the extraordinary familiarity of the Nestor of Indianists with the so-called classical language and literature, but we regret that, in the dictionary as in the grammar, he preferred to remain at the standpoint of the Indian scholars rather than to set out upon the path demanded by European scholarship. Our book too, despite all efforts, cannot wholly rise above the preparatory stage, but we nevertheless advance one step closer to the goal.

Our dictionary consists of two elements not entirely resolvable into one another: the material of the word-collections made by learned Indians, and our own collections drawn from Sanskrit literature. Had it been possible to exploit and work through all the works of Sanskrit literature that have come down to our time, then the material of the Indian word-collections — which is in any case poor and slight in proportion to the entire vocabulary of the language — might have been set aside without any considerable harm, and it would not have been necessary to repeat the countless errors and distortions which Indian scholarship presents to us. But since for this both the means and the resources were equally lacking, it lay within our task to supplement the deficiency of our own collections with the store of those, and in return to accept their defects into the bargain as well.

In the book itself one will find examples enough of the strange contradictions in which Indian lexicography stands with demonstrable usage; still more numerous are the cases in which it attains the truth only by half. And one can scarcely deny the probability of an error by pointing, say, to some other usage in another source not accessible to us or not taken into account by us, precisely because in the error there still lies an unmistakable pointing toward the truth. But just as, on the word of an ancient Brâhmaṇa, one believes what one sees oneself, and not the words of others — even of many — who assure the contrary, so here too the glance into the books before us must be more powerful than the testimony of the glossators.

We must record these contradictions and divergences, must attend to the residue of Indian lexicography that does not resolve into actual usage, even to the words evidently distorted by the glossator out of unfamiliarity with the texts, in order not to sweep out, here and there along with the rubble, a usable piece which we are not rich enough to do without.

It is from this point of view that we wish our procedure with the words and word-explanations handed down by the grammarians and lexicographers to be regarded.
