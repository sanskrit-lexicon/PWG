# FOREWORD.

*(Continuation of [pwgpref23](pwgpref23.md); the sentence begins there: „… If a word or a word-meaning already occurs in the Veda, this would have to be noted even if the Veda as such …“)*

… were excluded on principle, since no one may regard such a patent of nobility with indifferent eye. In the interest of the users of our dictionary, however, we should wish that the author of a concise dictionary marked everything new that he offers, and also everything old that he construes differently, by appending in such a case, by way of exception, the citations as well — even were this to be done at the expense of those for whom his work is primarily intended. Gildemeister, in the glossary to Lassen's Anthology, has marked with an asterisk words and meanings that are wanting in ours, for which we must thank him.

In a large dictionary a risky etymology or comparison does comparatively little harm, since it is as a rule used only by scholars; a concise dictionary is in the hands of beginners, to whom only what is entirely certain may be offered, so that they may in good time accustom themselves to circumspection and acquire the feeling for what is correct or probable — that feeling, so indispensable to the philologist and independent of all learning.

To append a transcription to the Sanskrit word is an evil, since it makes the book more expensive and brings no benefit. Whoever reads Sanskrit texts must know the Sanskrit alphabet; and whoever does not know the Sanskrit alphabet should not use the dictionary for comparative linguistics. In the field of Indo-Germanic comparative linguistics there are nowadays so many masters that we truly have no need of bunglers. Nor can a concise dictionary in general take the place of a large dictionary for comparative linguists, since it leaves out of account the grist and grain of a word.

The following words are addressed to lexicographers, grammarians, and systematic opponents. To retain, after the manner of the Indian grammarians, ऋ, ॠ, ऌ, ए, ऐ and ओ in roots, while one nevertheless readily abandons other peculiarities of theirs — such as, for example, the use of ण and ष at the beginning of roots that in reality begin with न and स — is in part an offence against the Indian grammarians, whom one makes appear more limited than they in reality were, but is altogether an offence against European scholarship, since one is, to be sure, able to cite Indian grammarians in justification of such spellings, while forgetting that they strove to do justice not to theory but to practice.

To impute to that Indian grammarian who first set up roots in ॠ and ओ that he believed in their actual existence is tantamount to assuming that he did not rightly understand how to distinguish a finished word from a stem (theme), since he sometimes calls the latter too (but for purely practical purposes) पद्. With the mere understanding of a rule of Pâṇini's one has not yet grasped Pâṇini's standpoint in scholarship. Nor does the study of the later grammarians lead us to this insight, but rather an unbiased philological intelligence does. If, for example, Pâṇini, in order to designate the case in which the irregular form आश्चर्य is formed from चर् with आ, says: आश्चर्यमनित्ये, i.e. one forms आश्चर्य when the word has the meaning «unusual», then only a pedant like Kâtjâjana can hit upon the idea of saying that his predecessor ought to have put अद्भुते *marvel* instead of अनित्ये. But if a European scholar attempts to reconcile the apparent contradiction between the two grammarians by assuming that the word आश्चर्य could, in the interval lying between Pâṇini and Kâtjâjana, have undergone a modification of meaning, then it appears as though this scholar were more competent to operate with mathematical quantities than with quantities whose exact value is to be determined only with philological tact. When now, to the grammarian's ingrained whims, there is added passion as well, to darken his insight, then abnormities arise, to oppose which cannot be expected of such people as are concerned with the matter itself.

If we look back on the fortunes our book has hitherto met with, we have every reason to be grateful for the recognition and for the effect which it has found, its weaknesses notwithstanding. The satisfaction we feel at this shall not be diminished for us by the fact that one person or another who uses us, and for whom we lighten the labour, does not indeed deem it necessary to acknowledge this, but the more eagerly seizes or contrives the opportunity to patch up some petty fault in our cloth, in order to set his own light all the more brightly upon the lampstand. Whoever takes credit to himself for that — let the vanity be granted him; we think there is room here for many, do not repay him in kind, but pass on by and continue our way undisturbed, a way which still lies long and toilsome enough before us.
