# FOREWORD.

The fifth part, which outwardly and inwardly seems to stand in contradiction to the four preceding parts, at the same time contains the conditions required for the redress of this: outwardly, in that — if any two of the preceding parts are combined into one volume — it can form by itself a fairly corresponding third volume; inwardly, in that, despite the considerable extent of the corrections and addenda, it does not make the work of looking things up more difficult but rather easier, since all the earlier corrections appended to the individual parts are from now on dispensable.

With the corrections and additions we believed that we ought to engage neither in a systematic working-through of texts that have become accessible in the meantime, nor in a thorough revision of extensive articles, so as not to delay significantly the progress and the completion of the work. We have rather confined ourselves to recording what we ourselves had gathered over the course of time, what could be drawn with comparatively little effort from the indices and dictionaries that have appeared in the meantime, and what was offered to us by good friends who think more of others than of themselves, such as Kern, Kuhn, Stenzler and Weber\*). Even with this imperfect achievement we hope to have rendered a service to the users of the dictionary.

In particular, we did not consider it fitting to take up the Vedic articles at the very moment when a detailed dictionary to the Vedic texts by Th. Aufrecht and a translation of the Ṛgveda by M. Müller are being announced. No one will hold it against us that we gladly yield precedence to others, who will have it easier than we did sixteen years ago, that we hold back corrections for the present, and that we wish both works really to appear soon, so that we may yet make use of their fruits for at least a part of our dictionary. Should it later, when we have reached the end, be permitted by time and circumstances, it will still not be too late to return to the difficulties of the Veda.

As regards the forward-pressing first half of the 5th part, we would only remark that, in choosing whether a word is to be written with ब or व, we let ourselves be guided by good manuscripts of Vedic texts and by the editions that have appeared at Bombay. The editions just mentioned are in this respect more reliable than those printed at Calcutta, for the reason that in Marathi ब and व are to this day precisely distinguished, whereas in Bengali these two sounds have coincided. The words बक्र, बकुल, बटु, बन्दि, बन्दिन्, बन्दी, बन्धुर, बन्धुल, बर्बर, बलात्कृ, बाष्प and विडाल have unfortunately come into their right only in the addenda.

We may take this occasion to express our views on how we conceive the relation of a concise dictionary (Handwörterbuch) to a comprehensive one, and how the one might draw benefit from the other.

If the man who, in composing a dictionary, pursues the unattainable goal of completeness is obliged to gather up everything new he finds even from the most remote fields, which he has entered only by chance, then the author of a concise dictionary will have to delimit his field precisely and to avoid any transgression of it.

The author of a comprehensive dictionary lays out his entire store of knowledge and keeps nothing back for himself; if the author of a concise dictionary knows no more than he was able to draw from the scanty material that he places before the reader's eyes, then his work is in a sorry state.

The author of a large dictionary is excused if, on his far-flung travels, he here and there overlooks even something close at hand; from the author of a concise dictionary one rightly demands that he master his small field completely and that, as appointed guide, he not desert the inexperienced traveller — the user of his work — precisely in the moment of need.

The most exact references are indispensable in a comprehensive dictionary; but to carry such references over into a concise dictionary brings no benefit. A general indication of the categories of writings in which a word is used in a given meaning would, however, be in place.
