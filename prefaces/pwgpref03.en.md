# Foreword, 2

[page IV]

FOREWORD.

On the other hand, we have striven to give our own collections a greater extent, and thereby to lay the only firm ground for lexicography. A special list at the end of the Foreword enumerates the printed and manuscript works which have been excerpted by us, partly in full, partly with a selection of the characteristic and more important matter, and partly only in passing.

In particular, we have sought to win the right beginning for our work through a complete treatment of the most important books of Vedic literature. With this an entirely new field is opened, from whose yield a hitherto scarcely suspected wealth flows into the Sanskrit vocabulary. And the gain is not limited to this increase alone; it is just as considerable for the understanding of the whole body of the language. From there run out the threads of the concepts, which one is able to follow in their interlacings only if one holds fast to their beginning. Or, where in later usage the fundamental conception of a word has been effaced, broken apart as it were into seemingly unconnected pieces, like a worn or shattered coin, there one may reach back into the treasure of the Veda to find still the full, undamaged stamp and the statement of the original value.

Many roots which later disappear entirely, rich word-families which have died out down to a few descendants, one finds here alive and flourishing, and through them one learns to understand their later offshoots as well. For this reason etymology too is advanced especially by the unlocking of the ancient language, and comparative philology ought to hold itself far more strictly than has hitherto been the case to the now demonstrable oldest stock of the vocabulary.

What the Greek lexicon would lack if it began with Herodotus and the great tragedians and left out of account everything older — that same thing, and much more, the greater the mass of ancient literature is, is lacking in the Sanskrit dictionary which sets aside the books gathered under the wide name of the Veda.

To be sure, what H. T. Colebrooke said fifty years ago has come to pass — that the difficulty of the Vedic language would long stand in the way of a more exact investigation drawing out from this extensive literature all that is worth knowing; but the labours of the last decades have nevertheless made this primeval forest passable to such a degree that the attempt at exploration undertaken by us will not remain without result. One might call this attempt a bold one, because a dictionary is to be created before exegesis has even seriously begun its work: one demands the conclusion and the result of a reckoning before the individual factors of it have been established. For it must be acknowledged that almost all the exegetical preliminary work that exists is more apt to mislead us than to lead us in difficult matters. With the exception of Th. Benfey's work on the Sâmaveda, nothing extensive has yet been brought to light that would satisfy the most general demands of a scientific exegesis, and even this predecessor we may not always follow. Our business would already be as good as half done if Langlois' alleged translation of the Ṛgveda deserved that name in any way. But it deserves it only for those passages in which it would be hard even for a beginner to go wrong, and scarcely even for these, since it takes pains everywhere to force the most perverse sense upon its text.

The spirit of the Veda, with its simple and lofty thoughts, was here forced through the range of ideas of an interpreter in whom it was unable to strike even a single spark — an interpreter who, weak in his knowledge of the language and visibly disdaining the necessary preparations, has lost many years upon a work that went far beyond his powers.

Were Langlois' book merely useless, then the bequest of the now departed might be set aside and covered with the veil of oblivion; but because in unskilled hands it works harmfully and must obscure and impede the understanding of Indian antiquity just as Anquetil's translation of the Zendavesta has impeded insight into the Iranian past, a decided testimony must be given against it: it must be denied each and every authority. And this testimony is needed, because men who might well have been quite familiar with the state of affairs have, for whatever reasons, not spoken this truth in their public judgments.

If only scanty aids for the understanding of Vedic texts are offered us by the moderns, we are all the more directed to that which Indian scholarship itself has done for their explanation — to the commentaries. And indeed, for the one of the two parts of Vedic literature, for the books of theology and of cult, we could wish for no better guides than these commentators, exact in every particular, who follow their texts word for word, who do not tire of repeating everywhere what has often already been said, wherever even a semblance of misunderstanding might arise, and who sometimes seem to have written rather for us strangers than for their own, grown up amid these conceptions and impressions,
