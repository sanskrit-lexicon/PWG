# Foreword, 3-1

FOREWORD.

The third volume of the dictionary, which according to our initial estimate was to contain also the letter न and to close the first half of the whole work, now appears nonetheless without न, since we did not wish to let it swell too greatly beyond the measure of the first two volumes. We have also convinced ourselves in the course of the work that every calculation made in advance concerning the extent of the whole work proves in the end to be incorrect. The assumption, then, that with the conclusion of न half of the entire labour would be accomplished may no longer be regarded as authoritative either. That we did not wish to close a volume in the middle of a letter will hardly require justification.

It was very vexing for us that we felt obliged to work up for the dictionary also such texts as, by their content and form, indeed deserved in high degree to be taken into account here, but which still offered a wide field to criticism and explanation. Among such texts are, among others, the sayings of Bhartṛhari and those in the Pañcatantra. For the first work we owe to Schiefner and Weber a most valuable collection of variants, with the help of which in most cases a thoroughly intelligible text adequate to the sense can be established. For the criticism and understanding of the Pañcatantra, Benfey's work has been of great importance to us; but the poetic part of the work, which forms a rich source for the dictionary, still requires—even after the remarks which one of us has published on Benfey's translation*)—much further assistance before one may boast of having penetrated to its full understanding. A great loss of time, accompanied at the same time by the danger of after all missing what is correct in the end, lies in the fact that one tries to settle the correct text each time, so to speak, at the very last moment.

The ill consequences of such a procedure have led one of the undersigned to the idea of establishing the text of the sayings of Bhartṛhari, in the first place for his own use, according to the available aids, and thereby easing his work on the dictionary. The publication of a text purified in this way might, however, find favour in a wider circle as well; and perhaps it would also not be inappropriate to incorporate into this collection the sayings of other Indian thinkers and poets. For the sake of ease of finding, the arrangement would have to be strictly alphabetical (according to the opening words of the saying); for each saying all the passages in which it is communicated would have to be indicated; in addition, it would be the duty of the editor to communicate also the variants, at least the more important ones. A translation of the sayings would surely also be in place, but above all a real-index as complete as possible, with the help of which one would be able to find any saying even from an incomplete recollection.

Our friend W. D. Whitney, whose assistance with the Vedic writings we already mentioned in the preface to the first volume, now also sends us contributions from the Sūrjasiddhānta, for which we are under great obligation to him.

Buddhist literature will from now on also be more fully taken into account, in consequence of the indefatigable cooperation of our colleague and friend A. Schiefner. The list of the writings newly drawn upon from this branch of literature is found under the "Explanation of the abbreviations newly added in the third volume."

[signed:]
OTTO BÖHTLINGK.
RUDOLPH ROTH.

St. Petersburg, / Tübingen, 1/13 July 1861.

[Fussnote / footnote:]

*) On this occasion we cannot refrain from declaring that at Pañkat. III, 98 we were in the wrong against Benfey: कौपीनम् is indeed the object and accordingly denotes *the private parts*. This passage is therefore to be placed in the dictionary under कौपीन 1. At I, 421, निः-सारात्ल्यफलानि [?] is surely, as Benfey translates, to be connected with कार्यापि. At III, 235, उपनतभये: was misunderstood both by B. and by us. One should translate: *Those who are threatened by danger must [take] every road that leads to salvation, be it exalted or common* [...] [continuation:] but rather *not held in regard*; Benfey renders it by *weak*. Several passages which we have not touched upon at all in our article will be discussed in another place; here our only concern was to make good as quickly as possible the wrong committed. B.
