# Foreword, 4-1

FOREWORD.

The compiler of a large dictionary faces a twofold danger: if he reaches too far afield and loses himself too much in particulars, or if he sets about the work only at an advanced age, it may easily happen that his work is left unfinished — and an unfinished dictionary is less useful than any other work that has not been brought to completion. The other danger is that, intent above all on actually bringing his work to an end, he hastens it too much at the expense of accuracy and completeness. If we take the 2nd edition of Wilson's dictionary as our measure, then ⅗ of our work has been accomplished; on this portion we have spent about 12½ years. At the same rate we should still require about 8½ years to complete the remaining ⅖ of the work — years which, by human reckoning, we may well hope to live. Into the first danger, then, we have not ventured, given the whole design of the work, and we have thereby escaped the reproach of having frivolously undertaken a work of unforeseeable end, for which we could not count on anyone to continue it. But the second danger too we have always kept before our eyes in our work, having remained faithful to the resolve we formed at the beginning of the undertaking, namely to devote a long succession of years entirely to the dictionary.

17/29 November 1864.

The shortcomings of our dictionary are very well known to us, but the awareness that we are working to the best of our ability, and that our work is used by hundreds and stimulates further research, gives us the courage and strength to press on undaunted along the difficult path.

We regret that Dr. H. Kern's removal to Benares has deprived us of his contributions for the letter क, but we gladly entertain the hope that this source will prove just as fruitful for the following letters as it has for the preceding ones.

To our friend W. D. Whitney we owe, besides the contributions already mentioned earlier, a list of the more important words from the Sanskrit inscriptions published in the Journal of the American Oriental Society.

On a particular occasion we draw attention to the fact that the participation of our friend A. Weber — to whom we are greatly indebted for communicating an ever-growing body of important and rich material, in part accessible to him alone — is not of such a kind that in every single view advanced by the dictionary regarding the passages he has communicated the expression of his own understanding may be seen.

O. Böhtlingk.
R. Roth.
