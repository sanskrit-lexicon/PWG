Even when practised according to linguistic laws, it moves in far too wide logical circles to hit the right point each time, and produces far too general, colourless concepts which perhaps contain within themselves the firmly circumscribed, sharply defined content of the word, but do not render it in its particularity and thereby in its force and beauty.

This is the procedure in which the commentators went before us, and with which they have furnished the surest proof that they had not mastered the entire vocabulary of these books at one and the same time, and likewise that they did not work from a fixed traditional explanation for the individual passages. Hence it comes about that they have explained a countless number of words of the Veda with *force, sacrifice, food, wisdom*, and numerous verbs, for example, with *to go, to move*, all of which, differing among themselves, have a definite value and concrete content and often scarcely even remotely brush against those general concepts. And only by reinstating these misunderstood words in their lost right does the Veda gain striking sense, force, and richness of expression, and give us a quite different picture of the thought-world of the earliest antiquity.

Whoever knows the difficulties of such an undertaking will not refuse us indulgence for our doubtless numerous missteps — missteps which, in the course of the work, will reveal themselves first and most clearly to ourselves. And this part of the dictionary, being the newest, will also be the first to become outdated; for the combined labour of many able workers directing themselves to the Veda will very rapidly advance the understanding of it and determine many things more truly and more exactly than we were able to do at the first attempt. Centuries have laboured at the lexical understanding of Homer, and yet his vocabulary is not exhaustively explained, and yet Homer presents, on the linguistic side, incomparably fewer difficulties than the Vedic hymn. How could one have hoped to recast without delay these monuments of an early antiquity — which confronts us nowhere else than here, fixed in writing — into translations, like some piece of modern book-making!

After a good copy of the Taittirîja-Saṁhitâ had been procured for us during the printing through the obliging efforts of Dr. Röer in Calcutta, we have been able to work through all the Vedic Saṁhitâ, and have taken care to exploit fully those of the Ṛk and the Atharvan, the Vâǵasaneji-Saṁhitâ, and the proper portions of the Sâmaveda, and also to record the compounds; a procedure which was not extended to the Taittirîja-Saṁhitâ, since in its main content it belongs not to the hymn collections but to the class of writings of the Brâhmaṇa, for which the same completeness of recording could not be achieved without taking up too much space for an inessential gain.

We receive a valuable support through a complete and exact word-index to the Atharvan, which Professor W. D. Whitney in New Haven, Connecticut, supplies to us.

The inclusion of the Brâhmaṇa and Sûtra literature in the considerable extent which it now occupies in the dictionary has become possible only because Dr. Albrecht Weber in Berlin furnished us with the rich material of the Çatapathabrâhmaṇa, of the Sûtra of Kâtjâjana, and subsequently also that of the Çâṅkhâjana and the Pâraskara. Without this assistance we would, at least for the beginning of the work, have been restricted to the Aitarejabrâhmaṇa, to the two Sûtra of Âçvalâjana, as well as to those of Kauçika belonging to the Atharvaveda. We also regard it as a pleasant duty to declare publicly that Weber, from the first moment until now, has with his characteristic warmth sought in every way to advance this undertaking begun with united forces.

Dr. Adalbert Kuhn has begun to exploit Çâṅkhâjana's Gṛhjasûtra for our dictionary.

Dr. Aufrecht, before his removal to Oxford, supported us in the Vedic domain up to अग्र excl.

To Professor Stenzler we must express our thanks for the relinquishing of a complete index to the Manu, which contains *all* words with the citation of *all* passages in which they appear; the same scholar has also communicated to us extracts from various unedited Indian dictionaries which are cited by the scholiasts of the Kirâtârguṇija and the Çiçupâlavadha, and finally has left to us for free use a copy of the first edition of Bopp's Glossary, in which rare words and word-meanings from various authors have occasionally been recorded.

To the Academician Schiefner we owe manifold communications from the Buddhist literature, in particular from the Vjutpatti, a Sanskrit-Tibetan dictionary.

The philosophical literature is most weakly represented in our work, and we would receive with particular thanks help in this direction from scholars who are at home in this field.
